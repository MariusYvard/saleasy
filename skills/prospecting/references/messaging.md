# Outreach messaging

Write outbound that earns replies. Personalize on a real reason, keep it short and run the quality gate before showing anything.

## Personalization decision gate
Generic AI personalization that just describes the company tends to do worse than none. Before inserting any AI context variable, test: does your value to the prospect change based on what they do. If yes, personalize on that. If no, stay static on one concrete observed signal. One sentence of true relevance beats three of praise.

## Openers (pick one)
Problem-sniffing: name a publicly observable gap. Billboard: whole offer in subject and first line so the right people self-select. Light context: one true detail, only if it passes the gate.

## Subject lines
Short. Question-format under about 42 characters tends to outperform. Lowercase personal subjects read human ("question for {first_name}"). Colleague test: could a colleague plausibly have sent this. Avoid "Quick question" and "Curious".

## Body discipline
Them-to-us ratio about 3 to 1. Use the "so you can focus on" pattern to tie a capability to an outcome. Numbers over adjectives, active voice, one idea. Three-pass cut: delete fluff, compress sentences, cut adjectives; target 50 to 90 words for a first email, shorter for a DM.

## Cadence (J0, J3, J7, J10)
J0 first touch: open on the dated signal, one line on the pain, one proof point, a low-friction ask. J3 follow-up: a new angle, not a repeat. J7 value-add: share something useful, no pitch. J10 clean close: a short break-up that leaves the door open. Adjust spacing to cycle and volume. Never send the same text on two channels. Thread J0 and the J3 reply with a blank subject; start J7 as a fresh thread with a new subject; front-load the strongest phrase for the preview.

## Healthy-cadence guardrails
Recommend and respect: a sane daily cap (roughly 15 to 20 cold LinkedIn invites), the prospect's working hours and timezone, deduplication (never the same person twice in a day, never two sequences at once across channels) and staggered sends.

## Deduplication across contacts at one company
One live thread per company at a time. When a company has two or more contacts (for example two buyers at the same firm), do not open both at once: pick the better-fit contact, start there and hold the second contact until the first thread stalls or the user says to multithread on purpose. When the user does choose to reach two contacts at one company, space the sends (different days, ideally different angles) so the company does not receive two near-identical emails the same morning. Before drafting a batch, group recipients by company domain and flag any company that appears more than once so the user decides the order.

## Signature
Connector-created drafts do not inherit the user's Gmail or Outlook signature, so embed it in the body of every email draft. Use the light inline HTML signature from the profile (name, role, company, phone, booking link). Never use an image signature or a tracking pixel: images hurt deliverability and often render as attachments or broken links in a drafted message. If the profile holds only a plain-text signature, wrap it in minimal HTML (line breaks plus the booking link as a real anchor). One signature per thread: include it on J0, then keep follow-ups in the same thread clean or use a one-line short signature.

## Quality gate
Before presenting any message, score it 0 to 10 on relevance, clarity, brevity, single ask, tone fit and deliverability. If any axis is below 7, rewrite once and re-score. Show only the version that clears the bar.

## Re-engaging stalled leads
Do not repeat the last message. Change the angle: a new signal, a different stakeholder, a useful resource. Space wider than the original cadence. After two attempts with no reply, mark the lead dormant.

## Reply and objection map
Interest: propose two slots, attach the booking link. Price: anchor on value and the proof metric, ask what outcome would justify the cost, never discount reflexively. Not now: agree, set a specific check-in, add a reminder. Wrong person: ask for a warm referral. No need: thank, leave the door open, mark dormant.

## Account-based motions
When a deal has several stakeholders, a competitor, a phone touch or a referral path: multithread by role (exec sponsor wants outcome and risk; economic buyer wants ROI; operational leader wants workflow impact; technical buyer wants fit and security; end user wants daily experience), shorter for senior roles. Arm a champion with a sixty-second pitch, a one-pager, an ROI snapshot and an objection sheet. Keep honest competitive battle cards with three displacement scripts ("also evaluating X", "already use X", "why you over X") and a never-say list. For phone: pattern-interrupt opener, a permission bridge, value in one line, a relevance-scale discovery bridge. For referrals: ask within about 48 hours of a stated win, be specific about who you want introduced, close the loop.

## Measurement
North-star: positive reply rate (positive replies over total sent, excluding out-of-office and bounces). Classify replies (interested, soft-yes, referral, not-now, not-a-fit, hostile, unsubscribe, OOO, bounce, auto, other); track hostile separately as a targeting risk. Run single-variable experiments: change list or copy or offer, never all at once; launch both arms together and read at about day 21; roughly 500 per arm for a large effect. Fold reply quality into the weekly review.
